Music in Movies

Music can serve several purposes that are either important on the
emotional side of the movie or help/enhance the storytelling. It is not
only helpful but essential for any director to keep the music in mind
when shooting the movie, as this can really make an average film or, a
fantastic one. Take Inception for example, it mostly known for the deep
intricate story about a group of people that incept ideas into people's
minds for whatever purposes they might have; yet the music is always
keeping you in the shoes of the characters, with suspencefull moments
having the audience clench their teeth for the characters to pull through.

Now compare it to John Carpenter's The Thing from 1982. Some may have
noticed that their is not a whole lot of music in the movie.the sound-scape
for it is usually filled with the characters talking, or their actions
on-screen. But there are subtle moments when the music does come in,
like the opening with the dog running in, and whenever a "thing" is
found out, or attacks a character. But the movie has you so focused on
the mystery of who could it be, that there is no need for that much
sound, (there is no need, really to me at least) as its absence is
filled with your own thoughts about the characters and who is a "thing"
or a human.

Music In Games

This is a great topic I am really excited about, as video games are a completely
different medium with music than TV shows (there is an exception here somewhat)
or movies can be. as movies just have you watching the events unfold, in games,
there is noone driving the main character(s) forward other than the player. With
this in mind, it is hard to really come up with the music as unlike movies and
TV, there is no point where the character (player) will do a thing at this exact
moment during gameplay. Because of this interactivity with the characters in
game, game developers don't precisly know what the player is doing at any givin
moment, thus the music could feel out of place if done incorrectly. There are
some exceptions and workarounds with this however; some devs just make games
called, (what many refer to as) walking simulators. Where the majority of the
game is just walking around beautifully designed environments to tell a story,
but have little to no real gameplay such as, Dear Esther, Gone Home, Everybody's
Gone to the Rapture, or Firewatch. Very stunning looking environments in these
games, but not much gameplay (all the player does is walk around looking for story),
which can be a nice bit of fresh air when playing. But the music is actually a
little more set up like a movie, or cutscenes that happen in a video game, where
the music is pretty much set the whole way through the game.


Those are some games that with the way the developers set them up, they could more
or less, make them an interactive movie, or, as close to a movie/Tv show as they could
for whatever their reasons where. But now we get to the good stuff. the games like
Dead Space, or Red Dead Redemption 2, Halo: Combat Evolved, the vast majority of games
fit here, be they a First-person shooter, a 3d platformer like Banjo Kazooie, or a
Virtual Reality game like Boneworks. These games all have the player that can be
unpredictable at various times, so how does the game developer know when the music
should change to make a person feel frightened, or ready to Rip and Tear? I'd like
to let this video share what I feel can help you understand how I veiw music in games,
and what they can be using "Adaptive music". Be aware, there is light swearing, and
